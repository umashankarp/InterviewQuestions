## Interview Questions

### Basic (10)

1. **Q: What is architecture trade-off analysis, at a high level?**
   **A:** The structured practice of comparing candidate architectural decisions against multiple, often-competing quality attributes (performance, availability, consistency, cost, maintainability, security, time-to-market) to make an explicit, justified choice — rather than defaulting to a single, familiar option or optimizing for one attribute while silently ignoring its cost to the others.
   **Why correct:** States the core practice (structured, multi-attribute comparison) and the failure mode it exists to prevent (single-attribute optimization, unexamined default choice).
   **Common mistakes:** Treating "trade-off analysis" as merely listing pros and cons informally, rather than a structured comparison against explicitly named quality attributes with an explicit, justified final decision.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why can't a 'best' architecture exist independent of context?" (Every quality attribute improvement generally costs something elsewhere — e.g., strong consistency costs availability under partition, per Module 47's CAP framing — so "best" is only meaningful relative to a specific system's specific, prioritized requirements.)

2. **Q: What is a quality attribute (also called a non-functional requirement), and why must it be made explicit before comparing architectures?**
   **A:** A quality attribute is a measurable system property beyond pure functional correctness — latency, availability, scalability, security, maintainability, cost — and it must be made explicit because architectures cannot be meaningfully compared against an unstated, assumed priority ordering; two engineers can disagree about "the right architecture" simply because they're silently assuming different, unstated quality-attribute priorities.
   **Why correct:** States what a quality attribute is and the specific reason (avoiding silent, unstated priority disagreement) it must be explicit.
   **Common mistakes:** Assuming quality attributes are "obviously" shared/understood across a team without ever writing them down, then discovering mid-debate that different stakeholders were silently optimizing for different things.
   **Follow-ups:** "Give an example of two quality attributes in direct tension." (Consistency and availability under network partition, Module 47; or development velocity/time-to-market and long-term maintainability, Module 105's monolith-vs-microservices framing.)

3. **Q: What is ATAM (Architecture Tradeoff Analysis Method), at a conceptual level?**
   **A:** A structured evaluation method where stakeholders explicitly state business drivers and quality-attribute priorities, architects present candidate architectural approaches, and the group systematically identifies each approach's *sensitivity points* (where a small change in one attribute significantly affects the design), *trade-off points* (where improving one attribute measurably worsens another), *risks*, and *non-risks* — producing a documented, shared understanding of exactly what each candidate architecture actually costs and buys, rather than an informal, undocumented group impression.
   **Why correct:** States ATAM's structured mechanism (explicit driver-gathering, sensitivity/trade-off/risk identification) and its output (a documented, shared understanding), distinguishing it from an informal discussion.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating ATAM as simply "a meeting where people discuss architecture," missing its specific, structured vocabulary (sensitivity points, trade-off points) that forces genuinely explicit, not merely impressionistic, analysis.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why does explicitly separating 'sensitivity point' from 'trade-off point' matter?" (A sensitivity point reveals where a decision is fragile to a single attribute's change; a trade-off point specifically reveals where two attributes move in opposite directions — conflating them would miss that some fragile decisions don't actually cost anything elsewhere, while others genuinely do.)

4. **Q: What is a decision matrix (weighted-scoring model), and how is it used in architecture trade-off analysis?**
   **A:** A structured table listing candidate architectures as rows and quality attributes as columns, with each cell scored (e.g., 1–5) for how well that architecture satisfies that attribute, each attribute given an explicit weight reflecting its relative priority, and a weighted total computed per candidate — making the comparison's actual reasoning inspectable and debatable, rather than hidden inside an individual's unexplained intuition.
   **Why correct:** States the concrete mechanism (scored table, explicit weights, computed total) and its purpose (making the comparison's reasoning inspectable, not hidden).
   **Common mistakes:** Treating the resulting number as an objective, final verdict rather than a structured aid whose real value is exposing and enabling debate over its underlying scores and weights — a wrong weight or a mis-scored cell produces a confidently-wrong total exactly as easily as no analysis at all.
   **Follow-ups:** "What's the risk of over-trusting a decision matrix's final score?" (It launders subjective judgment (the scores, the weights) into an apparently objective number, potentially shutting down the very debate the matrix should instead be provoking — Advanced Q7 develops this risk fully.)

5. **Q: What is the difference between a functional requirement and a quality attribute requirement, and why does confusing them lead to weak architecture decisions?**
   **A:** A functional requirement states *what* the system must do (process a payment, display a search result); a quality attribute requirement states *how well* it must do it under what conditions (process a payment within 200ms at the 99th percentile, remain available during a single-AZ failure) — confusing them leads to weak decisions because an architecture can satisfy every functional requirement while still catastrophically failing an unstated quality requirement (e.g., correctly processing payments, but only one at a time, serially).
   **Why correct:** States the precise distinction (what vs. how-well-under-what-conditions) and a concrete failure scenario illustrating why conflating them is dangerous.
   **Common mistakes:** Writing requirements documents that thoroughly specify functional behavior while leaving quality attributes as vague, unstated assumptions ("it should be fast," "it should be reliable") that no architecture decision can actually be measured against.
   **Follow-ups:** "How would you convert a vague quality requirement like 'it should be reliable' into something an architecture decision can actually be evaluated against?" (State it as a measurable target — e.g., a specific availability SLO (Module 94) and a specific failure scenario it must survive — converting an unmeasurable adjective into a concrete, checkable criterion.)

6. **Q: What is the "there is no silver bullet" principle in architecture decision-making, and how does it apply to choosing between the styles in Module 105?**
   **A:** No single architectural style or technology is universally superior across every context — a choice that's excellent for one system's actual constraints (team size, scale, consistency needs) can be actively harmful for another's, meaning "microservices are better than monoliths" (or vice versa) is a category error; Module 105 already established this specifically for architectural styles, and this module generalizes it into the standing principle underlying all architecture trade-off analysis.
   **Why correct:** States the principle (no universally superior choice, only context-appropriate ones) and connects it explicitly to Module 105's already-established, specific instance of it.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating a new, popular architectural pattern as unconditionally an improvement over an older one, rather than evaluating whether its specific trade-offs actually suit the system's specific, current context.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why do genuinely experienced engineers still sometimes disagree even after explicitly stating the same quality-attribute priorities?" (Reasonable disagreement can remain even with shared priorities, because estimating exactly how much a given architecture actually delivers on each attribute — not merely which attributes matter — still involves real, defensible uncertainty; Expert Q3 develops this further.)

7. **Q: What is a "reversible" versus "irreversible" architectural decision, and why does this distinction matter for how much upfront trade-off analysis a decision deserves?**
   **A:** A reversible decision can be changed later at a cost proportionate to ordinary engineering effort (a caching strategy, an internal API's shape); an irreversible (or very-expensive-to-reverse) decision commits the organization to a costly, risky migration if it later proves wrong (a foundational data-storage technology, a core service-boundary decision) — the more irreversible a decision, the more upfront trade-off analysis rigor it warrants, directly Module 106's "last responsible moment" principle applied specifically to deciding how much analysis effort a given decision deserves.
   **Why correct:** States the distinction and connects the resulting analysis-effort calibration explicitly to Module 106's already-established decision-timing principle.
   **Common mistakes:** Applying uniform, heavyweight trade-off analysis rigor to every architectural decision regardless of its actual reversibility, wasting analysis effort on cheaply-reversible decisions while potentially under-analyzing a genuinely hard-to-reverse one.
   **Follow-ups:** "Give an example of a decision that looks irreversible but is actually cheaply reversible given this module's migration patterns (Module 107)." (A data-store choice, historically treated as a one-way door, is meaningfully more reversible once CDC-based migration and dual-write reconciliation (Module 107) are available as a genuine, verified path to change it later — reducing, though not eliminating, its true irreversibility.)

8. **Q: What is the difference between a Principal Engineer's role in an architecture decision and an individual contributor's, at a high level?**
   **A:** An individual contributor typically evaluates a decision's technical merits within a bounded, already-scoped problem; a Principal Engineer additionally weighs the decision's business impact, cross-team consequences, organizational cost/risk, and long-term maintainability trade-offs, and is responsible for ensuring the decision-making *process* itself (not just the final choice) is sound, explicit, and durably recorded — extending technical judgment into organizational and temporal scope an individual contributor's typically-narrower mandate doesn't require.
   **Why correct:** States the specific scope expansion (business/cross-team/organizational/long-term, plus responsibility for the decision *process*) distinguishing the two roles.
   **Common mistakes:** Reducing the Principal-level distinction to "makes bigger technical decisions," missing that the more fundamental difference is scope of consequence and responsibility for the decision-making process's own soundness, not merely the decision's technical difficulty.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why does 'responsibility for the decision-making process itself' matter, beyond just making a good individual decision?" (A sound process (explicit quality attributes, documented trade-offs, an ADR) produces decisions that remain defensible, auditable, and correctly revisitable later — even a good decision made through an undocumented, ad hoc process leaves the organization unable to later understand or reconsider it soundly.)

9. **Q: What is cost as a quality attribute in architecture trade-off analysis, and why is it often under-weighted relative to purely technical attributes?**
   **A:** Cost — infrastructure spend, operational/on-call burden, engineering time to build and maintain — is itself a genuine quality attribute directly trading off against others (a highly available, globally-distributed architecture costs substantially more than a simpler, single-region one), but it's often under-weighted because it's less viscerally interesting to engineers than latency or scalability, and its true, fully-loaded cost (including ongoing operational burden, not just infrastructure line-items) is frequently invisible at decision time.
   **Why correct:** States why cost qualifies as a genuine quality attribute and the specific reason (less technically interesting, true cost often invisible) it's commonly under-weighted.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating cost as an afterthought constraint applied only after the "real" technical trade-off analysis is complete, rather than as a first-class quality attribute weighed alongside the others from the outset.
   **Follow-ups:** "What's the risk of only counting infrastructure spend and ignoring operational/on-call cost when comparing architectures?" (An architecture that looks cheaper on an infrastructure bill can be dramatically more expensive once its ongoing operational burden — more services to monitor, more failure modes to diagnose, more on-call pages — is honestly accounted for; Module 92/96's operational-burden findings apply directly here.)

10. **Q: What is the role of an Architecture Decision Record (Module 106) specifically within the trade-off analysis process, as the capstone connecting this module back to Module 106?**
    **A:** The ADR is the durable artifact recording a trade-off analysis's actual output — the quality attributes considered, the candidates compared, the sensitivity/trade-off points identified, and the final, justified decision — converting what would otherwise be a one-time discussion whose reasoning is quickly forgotten into a searchable, revisitable record future engineers (and the same engineers, later) can consult rather than re-litigating the same analysis from scratch.
    **Why correct:** Directly connects this module's trade-off-analysis output to Module 106's already-established ADR mechanism as its natural, durable recording vehicle.
    **Common mistakes:** Conducting a rigorous, explicit trade-off analysis but never actually recording its reasoning in a durable, searchable ADR, losing most of the analysis's long-term value the moment the discussion that produced it is over and participants' memories fade.
    **Follow-ups:** "Why is capturing the *rejected* candidates and *why* they were rejected just as important as recording the chosen one?" (Module 106 Intermediate Q3's principle exactly — a future engineer facing renewed pressure to reconsider a rejected option needs to know it was already considered and why it lost, avoiding a costly, uninformed re-litigation of an already-settled question.)

### Intermediate (10)

1. **Q: Walk through conducting a lightweight ATAM-style trade-off analysis for choosing between a relational database and a NoSQL document store for a new order-management service.**
   **A:** (1) Explicitly gather business drivers and quality-attribute priorities from stakeholders — e.g., strong consistency for financial correctness is non-negotiable, sub-100ms read latency at high scale is important, flexible schema evolution is a nice-to-have; (2) present both candidates' actual characteristics against these priorities — the relational store offers strong ACID transactions but requires more schema-migration discipline (Module 20), the document store offers schema flexibility but weaker native multi-record transactional guarantees (Module 23/24); (3) identify the trade-off point — choosing the document store trades transactional strength for schema flexibility, directly conflicting with the stated "financial correctness non-negotiable" driver; (4) identify this as a clear risk requiring either rejecting the document store or introducing compensating mechanisms (application-level transactional patterns, Module 36's Saga); (5) record the resulting decision and reasoning in an ADR (Basic Q10).
   **Why correct:** Provides a concrete, five-step walkthrough (gather drivers, present candidates against them, identify the specific trade-off point, resolve/flag the risk, record the ADR) applied to a realistic, specific scenario.
   **Common mistakes:** Comparing the two data stores' general reputations or popularity rather than each candidate's actual characteristics against *this specific system's* explicitly-stated priorities — the "right" answer depends entirely on which quality attributes this particular system actually prioritizes, not a general ranking of database technologies.
   **Follow-ups:** "How would this analysis change if the system were an analytics/reporting service instead of order management?" (Strong per-transaction consistency would likely drop in priority relative to flexible, evolving schema and horizontal read scalability — the same two candidates, evaluated against different, legitimately different priorities for a different system, would very plausibly produce the opposite recommendation.)

2. **Q: How do you handle a situation where different stakeholders genuinely, legitimately disagree about quality-attribute priorities (e.g., Product wants fastest time-to-market, Platform wants long-term maintainability)?**
   **A:** Make the disagreement explicit rather than letting it remain an unstated, silent tension — present the trade-off concretely (e.g., "shipping in 6 weeks with approach A costs an estimated 3 additional months of technical debt remediation later; approach B ships in 10 weeks with minimal debt") so the decision becomes an informed, business-level choice made by whoever has the appropriate authority (often a Principal Engineer or EM weighing both inputs), rather than either side's priority silently winning by default or by whoever argues loudest.
   **Why correct:** States the concrete resolution mechanism (make the trade-off's actual cost explicit and quantified, escalate to appropriate decision authority) rather than assuming disagreement must simply be argued to consensus.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating stakeholder disagreement as a sign the analysis itself is flawed or incomplete, rather than recognizing that genuine, legitimate priority differences can exist even given a perfectly sound analysis — the analysis's job is to make the trade-off explicit and quantified, not to manufacture false consensus.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why is quantifying the trade-off (e.g., '3 months of later debt') more useful than simply stating the qualitative disagreement?" (A quantified estimate gives the deciding authority an actual basis for the business judgment call being made, rather than an abstract, hard-to-weigh "speed vs. quality" framing that provides no real handle on the decision's actual magnitude.)

3. **Q: What is a sensitivity point (ATAM), and how would you identify one in a real architecture, using the caching discussion from Module 103 as a concrete example?**
   **A:** A sensitivity point is a specific design parameter where a small change produces an outsized effect on a quality attribute — in Module 103's caching example, cache TTL is a sensitivity point: a small TTL increase can produce an outsized read-latency improvement (more cache hits) but also an outsized staleness-risk increase (more time serving stale data) — identifying it means recognizing this parameter, specifically, is where the architecture's quality-attribute behavior is disproportionately concentrated, warranting deliberate, careful tuning rather than an arbitrary default value.
   **Why correct:** States the ATAM definition precisely and grounds it in a concrete, already-covered example (cache TTL) rather than an abstract description.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating every design parameter as equally deserving careful tuning attention, rather than specifically identifying the few sensitivity points where tuning effort yields disproportionate quality-attribute impact.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why does identifying a sensitivity point matter for where you'd place a fitness function (Module 106)?" (A sensitivity point is exactly where continuous, automated monitoring/verification delivers the highest value — a parameter with outsized quality-attribute impact is precisely where undetected drift would be most consequential.)

4. **Q: How would you incorporate migration cost and risk (Module 107) into a trade-off analysis comparing the current architecture against a proposed new one?**
   **A:** Treat "cost and risk of getting from here to there" as its own explicit quality-attribute-like dimension in the comparison, not merely an afterthought once the "better" architecture is chosen — a proposed architecture that scores better on every steady-state quality attribute can still be the wrong choice if its migration cost/risk (using Module 107's decision framework: scope, data involvement, reversibility, organizational span) is disproportionate to the improvement gained, especially for a system where the current architecture, while imperfect, is not causing acute, pressing pain.
   **Why correct:** Explicitly elevates migration cost/risk to a first-class dimension of the comparison, directly reusing Module 107's own decision framework rather than treating migration as a separate, later concern.
   **Common mistakes:** Evaluating only the destination architecture's steady-state quality attributes, treating the migration itself as a "mere implementation detail" to be worked out later — separately from, rather than genuinely part of, the trade-off decision.
   **Follow-ups:** "Under what condition would a system with clearly worse steady-state quality attributes still be the better overall choice?" (When the current architecture's actual, measured pain is low (Module 105's last-responsible-moment framing) and the proposed migration's cost/risk is high relative to that modest pain — the "better" architecture in isolation is not necessarily the better decision once genuine migration cost is honestly weighed.)

5. **Q: What is opportunity cost, and why must it be explicitly considered in an architecture trade-off analysis, not just the direct cost of each candidate?**
   **A:** Opportunity cost is the value of what the team could have built or improved instead, had they not spent the time/resources on this architectural change — a technically-sound, well-justified architecture investment can still be the wrong decision if the team's limited capacity would have delivered more total business value applied elsewhere; explicitly considering it prevents evaluating an architecture decision as though it were made in a vacuum with unlimited engineering capacity.
   **Why correct:** States the definition (value of the foregone alternative use of capacity) and the specific reason it must be explicit (capacity is finite, decisions compete for it, not just for their own direct merits).
   **Common mistakes:** Justifying an architecture investment purely on "is this a good idea in isolation," without weighing it against what else the same engineering capacity could otherwise have accomplished during the same period.
   **Follow-ups:** "How would you concretely estimate opportunity cost for a proposed migration?" (Identify the highest-value alternative initiative the same team/capacity would otherwise pursue during the migration's timeline, and its estimated business value, providing a genuine comparison point rather than evaluating the migration against an implicit, unstated "or nothing" alternative.)

6. **Q: How would you structure a trade-off analysis discussion to avoid it collapsing into a single, dominant voice's unchallenged opinion (a "HiPPO" — highest-paid-person's-opinion — decision)?**
   **A:** Use ATAM's structure specifically to counter this risk — require every candidate's quality-attribute scores and every identified trade-off/sensitivity point to be explicitly stated and defended with concrete reasoning (not merely asserted), invite dissenting views deliberately before the group converges, and record the *disagreement*, not just the final decision, in the resulting ADR — a structured, explicit process makes an unexamined HiPPO decision visibly indefensible in a way an informal discussion doesn't.
   **Why correct:** States concrete structural countermeasures (explicit defended reasoning, deliberately-invited dissent, recorded disagreement) rather than merely asserting "avoid HiPPO decisions" as an unsupported goal.
   **Common mistakes:** Assuming a nominally "open discussion" format alone prevents a HiPPO outcome, without the specific structural discipline (mandatory explicit reasoning, deliberately solicited dissent) that actually counters the natural social dynamic of deferring to the most senior or most confident voice in the room.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why does recording dissent in the ADR matter, even once a decision is made?" (A recorded, reasoned dissent preserves the fact that a real, considered alternative view existed — valuable if the decision is later revisited, and a genuine signal to future readers that the decision wasn't unanimous or uncontested, unlike Module 106's "why" preservation for rejected options generally.)

7. **Q: What is the risk of treating an architecture trade-off analysis as a one-time event rather than something occasionally revisited, connecting to Module 106's evolutionary-architecture framing?**
   **A:** The quality-attribute priorities and candidate architectures' actual, comparative merits are both context-dependent and can genuinely change over time (a system's scale, team size, or business priorities shift) — a trade-off analysis conducted once, years ago, under different conditions, can silently become stale and no longer reflect the actual, current best decision, exactly Module 106's evolutionary-architecture principle that architecture is never a one-time, finished decision but a continuously-revisited one.
   **Why correct:** Directly connects the staleness risk to Module 106's already-established evolutionary-architecture principle, rather than treating it as a new, unrelated concern.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating a well-documented, rigorously-conducted trade-off analysis as permanently settled and beyond reconsideration, rather than recognizing its validity is conditioned on the context (scale, priorities, team) that produced it, which can itself change.
   **Follow-ups:** "What would trigger a legitimate re-analysis, versus an unwarranted, disruptive re-litigation of an already-settled decision?" (A genuine, material change in the underlying context the original analysis assumed — e.g., a 10x scale increase, a materially different regulatory requirement — versus merely a new engineer's unexamined personal preference for a different, currently-fashionable approach; Module 106 §Intermediate Q3's ADR-worthiness heuristic applies analogously here.)

8. **Q: How would you weigh security (Module 97–100) as a quality attribute against other attributes in a trade-off analysis, given this course's finding that security controls are often invisibly absent rather than visibly weak?**
   **A:** Because a missing security control (Module 97's IDOR finding) typically produces zero functional symptom and no visible signal it's absent, security must be explicitly, deliberately included as a named quality attribute in every trade-off analysis rather than assumed present by default — an architecture that scores well on performance/cost/maintainability but was never actually evaluated against explicit security requirements can silently carry a severe, invisible gap that none of the other, more visible quality attributes would ever surface.
   **Why correct:** Directly connects this module's general "quality attributes must be explicit" principle to security specifically, citing the concrete reason (invisible-when-absent) security is uniquely at risk of being silently omitted from the analysis.
   **Common mistakes:** Treating security as "handled separately" by a dedicated security review process disconnected from the main architecture trade-off analysis, rather than as a first-class quality attribute weighed alongside performance/cost/maintainability from the outset of the same analysis.
   **Follow-ups:** "Why might security specifically deserve threat-modeling (Module 97) as a complementary technique alongside ATAM's general sensitivity/trade-off-point analysis?" (ATAM's general framework identifies *known*, already-named attribute trade-offs; STRIDE-based threat modeling is specifically designed to surface novel, not-yet-considered attack-surface risks a general quality-attribute comparison wouldn't be structured to find on its own.)

9. **Q: How would you present a completed architecture trade-off analysis to non-technical executive stakeholders needing to approve budget/timeline, extending Module 106 Advanced Q9's evidence-based-communication principle?**
   **A:** Translate the analysis into business terms these stakeholders directly care about — cost (both direct infrastructure and total engineering time), risk (using Module 107's migration-risk framing: what specifically could go wrong, and what's the blast radius if it does), and timeline, with the underlying quality-attribute trade-offs presented as concrete business consequences (e.g., "choosing approach A ships 2 months faster but carries a specific, named risk of X-hour outages under Y scenario") rather than abstract technical vocabulary (sensitivity points, trade-off points) executives have no framework to evaluate.
   **Why correct:** Directly reapplies Module 106's already-established evidence-based, business-framed communication principle, specifically to trade-off-analysis outputs.
   **Common mistakes:** Presenting the raw ATAM vocabulary and decision-matrix scores directly to non-technical stakeholders, expecting them to translate the technical framework into business consequences themselves rather than doing that translation for them.
   **Follow-ups:** "What's the risk of presenting only the recommended option without also summarizing what was rejected and why?" (Executives lose visibility into what alternatives were genuinely considered and the reasoning behind ruling them out — the same "preserve the rejected reasoning" principle Basic Q10 established for ADRs generally, now applied to executive communication specifically.)

10. **Q: What role does an Architecture Review Board (Module 106) play specifically in a formal trade-off analysis, and when should its involvement be required versus optional?**
    **A:** Directly Module 106's already-established scoping principle — Board involvement should be required specifically for decisions with genuine, significant, hard-to-reverse, cross-team consequence (Basic Q7's irreversibility criterion), where the Board's broader, more experienced perspective adds real value beyond what the proposing team alone can assess; for a routine, reversible, single-team-scoped decision, requiring Board review would recreate the exact unnecessary bottleneck Module 106 already identified as a risk, with the team's own lightweight ATAM-style analysis and ADR being sufficient without escalation.
    **Why correct:** Directly reapplies Module 106's already-established Board-scoping criteria to trade-off analysis specifically, rather than treating this as a new, unrelated governance question.
    **Common mistakes:** Requiring Board review for every architecture trade-off analysis regardless of the decision's actual significance and reversibility, recreating Module 106's already-identified bottleneck risk in this specific context.
    **Follow-ups:** "What's the risk of the opposite failure — never escalating to the Board even for a genuinely significant, cross-team decision?" (A team's locally-optimal decision can impose real, unconsidered costs on other teams or the organization's broader architecture — exactly the disconnected-governance risk Module 106 Intermediate Q6 identified, now manifesting as a trade-off analysis conducted with too narrow a scope of stakeholders.)

### Advanced (10)

1. **Q: Conduct a full trade-off analysis for a Principal-level decision: whether to migrate a monolith's order-fulfillment module to an independent microservice, synthesizing Modules 105–107.**
   **A:** (1) Gather explicit quality-attribute priorities: current pain points (Module 105's diagnostic criteria — is this module genuinely a deployment bottleneck, or does it just "feel" coupled?), target scalability/availability needs, and organizational context (does a dedicated team already own this capability, per Conway's Law); (2) if Module 105's actual, empirically-confirmed distributed-monolith risk criteria are absent (no real deployment-coordination pain, no genuine team-ownership mismatch), the trade-off likely favors staying monolithic or modularizing internally first, per Module 105's modular-monolith-first default; (3) if genuine pain is confirmed, evaluate migration cost/risk using Module 107's four-axis framework (scope, data involvement, reversibility, organizational span) — a well-isolated module with its own already-distinct data ownership scores favorably; (4) design the actual migration path using Module 107's patterns (Strangler Fig routing, an ACL, CDC-based data migration with reconciliation); (5) establish fitness functions (Module 106) verifying the resulting service boundary's coupling stays low post-migration, not merely at the moment of cutover; (6) record the full analysis, chosen path, and rejected alternatives in an ADR.
   **Why correct:** Synthesizes all three prior modules' specific frameworks (105's diagnostic criteria, 107's migration-decision axes and patterns, 106's fitness-function verification and ADR governance) into one coherent, sequenced analysis rather than treating them as separate, disconnected concerns.
   **Common mistakes:** Jumping directly to "how do we migrate" (Module 107) without first rigorously confirming, via Module 105's actual diagnostic criteria, that migration is genuinely warranted at all — skipping the "should we" question in eagerness to answer "how."
   **Follow-ups:** "Why must step (5)'s fitness function specifically check coupling *after*, not just during, the migration?" (A migration can achieve a clean boundary at the moment of cutover, but new code added afterward can silently reintroduce coupling over time (Module 105's original distributed-monolith risk) unless continuously, not just once, verified — Module 106's core recurring finding, applied here.)

2. **Q: Critique a trade-off analysis that thoroughly compares candidate architectures' steady-state quality attributes but treats the migration path as "an implementation detail to figure out later."**
   **A:** This is an incomplete analysis by Intermediate Q4's own standard — migration cost/risk is not a separable implementation detail but a first-class dimension of the actual decision, since a proposed architecture that's clearly superior in steady state can still be the objectively wrong choice once a genuinely difficult, high-risk migration path (Module 107's reversibility axis) is honestly priced in; deferring this analysis "to later" specifically risks discovering the migration is far more costly or risky than assumed only after the destination architecture has already been organizationally committed to, precisely the kind of premature, under-informed commitment Module 106 Basic Q7's big-design-up-front critique warns against.
   **Why correct:** Directly identifies the specific flaw (treating a first-class decision dimension as a deferred afterthought) and connects the resulting risk explicitly to Module 106's already-established BDUF critique.
   **Common mistakes:** Assuming a superior destination architecture is self-evidently worth pursuing regardless of migration difficulty, only discovering the migration's true cost/risk after already committing to the decision organizationally (announcing it, allocating budget) — a much more expensive point to discover this than during the original analysis.
   **Follow-ups:** "What's a concrete way to price migration risk into the trade-off analysis at decision time rather than after?" (Explicitly run Module 107's four-axis migration-risk assessment (scope, data involvement, reversibility, organizational span) as a mandatory input to the same decision matrix (Basic Q4) used for steady-state quality attributes, not a separate, later, and easily-deprioritized exercise.)

3. **Q: How would you evaluate whether a proposed architecture change is being driven by genuine business need versus resume-driven development / technology fashion?**
   **A:** Trace the proposal back to an explicit, named quality-attribute or business driver (Basic Q2) and ask whether the current architecture is genuinely, measurably failing to meet that driver (using concrete evidence — an actual SLO breach, Module 94; an actual deployment-coordination bottleneck, Module 105's diagnostic criteria) — if the strongest justification offered is the new technology's general reputation or popularity rather than a specific, measured gap in the current system's actual behavior, this is a strong signal the proposal is fashion-driven rather than need-driven, and should be evaluated with correspondingly higher skepticism per Basic Q6's "no silver bullet" principle.
   **Why correct:** States a concrete diagnostic test (can the proposal be traced to a specific, measured current gap, or only to the new technology's general reputation) distinguishing genuine need from fashion-driven proposals.
   **Common mistakes:** Evaluating a proposed technology change purely on its own claimed merits without first checking whether the current architecture is actually, measurably failing to meet a real, specific requirement — a technically excellent technology can still be an unjustified change if nothing concrete is actually broken.
   **Follow-ups:** "Is resume-driven development always purely bad, with zero organizational value?" (Not entirely — genuine engineer growth and retention have real organizational value, and a well-justified trade-off analysis can sometimes legitimately weigh 'team skill development' as one minor, explicitly-named factor among others; the risk is specifically when it's the *unstated*, real driver disguised behind post-hoc technical justification, rather than an explicit, honestly-weighed factor.)

4. **Q: Design a trade-off-analysis approach for a decision where quantitative data is largely unavailable (e.g., a genuinely novel system with no comparable historical traffic/usage data).**

5. **Q: How would you detect that a decision matrix (Basic Q4) is being used to launder a predetermined conclusion rather than genuinely inform the decision?**
   **A:** Check whether the weights and scores were set *before* or *after* the preferred answer was already known — a matrix constructed by first deciding the desired outcome and then reverse-engineering weights/scores to justify it will typically show suspiciously convenient patterns (e.g., attributes favoring the preferred option weighted unusually high, attributes favoring alternatives conveniently omitted or scored generously despite weak actual evidence); a genuine matrix's weights should be set and defended based on the business drivers alone, before any candidate's scores are even computed, and should survive an independent stakeholder's separate weighting producing a similar overall conclusion.
   **Why correct:** States a concrete diagnostic (were weights set before or after the preferred answer was known; do independently-set weights converge on a similar conclusion) for detecting reverse-engineered analysis.
   **Common mistakes:** Accepting a decision matrix's final score as legitimate simply because a structured-looking table was produced, without checking whether its weights were genuinely set independent of the desired conclusion.
